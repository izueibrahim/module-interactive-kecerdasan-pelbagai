3.2.1

Bahasa
Tell Me About It

1. Read-Aloud!

  • Teacher models reading and pupils repeat after.
  • Pupils read aloud the story. Different pupils read dialogues in different voices of the characters.

Verbal-linguistic connects high verbal-linguistic intelligence with words and languages. These pupils are typically good at reading, writing, telling stories and memorising words. They enjoy debates, storytelling, online communications, group discussions and word games. They can work on jigsaw reading, and read-alouds.

2. Jazz It Up!

  • Teacher picks a few target words and phrases from the story to create a jazz chant.
  • Pupils clap their hands, stomp their feet, or they can even play percussions made of water bottles as they read the jazz chant!

The musical-rhythmic relates to sensitivity to sounds, rhythms, tones and music. Writing jazz chants would help pupils to remember vocabulary, tonal/rhythmic patterns in music of the target language. Songs, rhymes and chants can contribute to the pupils' global development, and acting together is fun and stimulates the pupils' sense of humour.

3. Role-Play!

  • Pupils 'become' anyone they like for a short time!
  • They choose and act out the role of their favourite character from the story:
    1. Taro
    2. Grandmother
    3. Village headman
    4. Warlord
    5. Several villagers (6 pupils)
    6. Several old people (4 pupils)
  • Teachers can adapt this activity to suit the story that they have chosen based on the level of their pupils

Bodily-kinesthetic, includes a sense of physical action, which is learning through movement and manipulating things. Some activities include role playing, dancing, total physical response (TPR), hands-on learning, manipulatives, and multimedia games or activities. Additional activities are known as stand-up and sit-down and inside/outside circle.

1. Making Props!

  • Some pupils prefer to work independently.
  • Teacher asks these pupils to prepare some props for the role-play such as:
    1. Trees, domestic and farm animals.
    2. Costumes – using recycled materials such as old newspapers, plastics, old fabrics, old straw, hoe, etc.
    3. Background scenes/art.

Intrapersonal pupils are the most independent of the learners. Some activities for these pupils include working with books, creating materials, writing diaries in private at their own time. These pupils work better independently before they share their work.

2. Making Slide Show!

  • Pupils who are good at computer skills can create slide shows out of the story!
  • Teachers can ask these pupils to transform the story into infographics.

Visual-spatial deals with spatial judgment and the ability to visualise the stories in their minds. The visual and spatial learner learns through the use of the eye. Some materials that will help them include using graphs and diagrams, drawing a response, video exercises, computer slide shows, multimedia projects, mind mapping, and graphic organisers. Other activities and materials include vocabulary cards, writing frames, journals, portfolios, sequencing mapping and scaffolding charts.

3. Role-Play!

  • Teachers can create online puzzles (e.g. Puzzlemaker) to gauge pupils’ understanding of the story.
  • Teachers can ask pupils to solve a puzzle by sequencing the plot of the story or even come up with their own puzzle!

Logical-mathematical includes logic, abstractions, reasoning, numbers and critical thinking. Activities include word order activities, grammar relationships, pattern games, number activities, classifying and categorising, sequencing information and computer games.
